City as Interface

The more I practice this kind of slow looking, the more the city feels like something I can actually read. It is not just a place I move through on my way to class or work. It becomes a system that I can understand and question. I start to notice which signals help people and which ones limit them. I notice where the city invites movement and where it blocks it. I notice the difference between official instructions and the paths people create on their own.


Paying attention to these signals does not solve every problem in the city, but it does change the way I experience it. I feel more connected to the spaces I walk through every day. I feel more aware of the choices that were made long before I arrived. Most of all, I feel more curious. There is always another sign, another mark, another small clue about how the city works. Once you start noticing them, it becomes hard to stop.
